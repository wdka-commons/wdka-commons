Community Libraries & Community Kitchens

Community kitchens and libraries are more than spaces for food or books; they are infrastructures of care, collective knowledge, and resistance. They operate against systems where access to sustenance, education, and culture is increasingly mediated by profit, bureaucracy, and exclusivity. By creating shared spaces for cooking, eating, reading, and learning, these initiatives challenge the commodification of everyday life and assert the social value of collective practice.

At their core, these projects imagine society as a network of reciprocal relations rather than a marketplace of isolated individuals. Community kitchens such as Snackbar Frieda or Swamp Rotterdam redistribute not only food but also skills, time, and attention, fostering relationships across social, economic, and generational divides.

Similarly, community libraries like Leeszaal West and the Social Practice Library reclaim knowledge from institutions and markets that gatekeep cultural and intellectual resources. They allow anyone to participate, borrow, contribute, or simply spend time, emphasising access over ownership and collective care over institutional authority.

The political significance of these spaces lies in their dual role: meeting immediate material and cultural needs while modelling alternative ways of living and learning. They encourage experimentation, collective authorship, and peer-to-peer pedagogy, functioning not only as sites of service but as laboratories for social imagination.
